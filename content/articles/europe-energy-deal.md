The European Union and Qatar signed a 25-year liquefied natural gas supply agreement on Tuesday, locking in deliveries through 2051 and providing what officials described as "long-term certainty" for the bloc's energy security.

The deal, signed in Doha by EU Energy Commissioner and Qatari Minister of Energy, covers an estimated **18 million tonnes per year** — roughly 12% of the EU's projected gas demand at the end of the decade.

## A complicated win

European negotiators framed the agreement as the final step in weaning the bloc off Russian pipeline gas. Combined with new LNG terminals in Germany, the Netherlands, and Italy, EU gas storage has reached **95% capacity ahead of winter** — a record for this point in the year.

But climate advocates have questioned the wisdom of locking in fossil supply for a quarter-century when the bloc's own roadmap calls for a 90% emissions cut by 2040.

> "We're not buying flexibility, we're buying lock-in," argued one MEP from the Green group. "Every euro we commit to a 2051 contract is a euro we're not investing in renewables and grid storage."

## Industry view

The deal landed favourably with European industry. Heavy manufacturers — particularly chemicals and steel — have lobbied hard for predictable long-term gas supply at competitive prices, arguing that uncertainty has driven investment away from the continent.

Analysts at Wood Mackenzie estimate the Qatar agreement will price LNG **15–20% below current spot levels**, providing significant relief to industrial consumers.

Whether that benefit outweighs the climate cost is now a question for Brussels — and for European voters heading into next year's parliamentary elections.
